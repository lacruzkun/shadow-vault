## 1. What role does society play?

Society isn't background noise — it actively shapes outcomes through:

- **Family & community support** — stable homes, mentors, role models
- **Education systems** — access to quality schools, teachers who believe in students
- **Economic opportunity** — jobs, scholarships, safety nets that let teens take risks
- **Social norms & expectations** — communities that expect success tend to produce it
- **Peer environment** — friend groups either reinforce ambition or normalize giving up

**Core thesis:** Teenagers don't succeed in a vacuum — society provides the scaffolding (or removes it).

**Key evidence:**
- A meta-analysis of 74 studies (101,000+ students, 6,871 schools) found a strong, consistent link between family socioeconomic status and academic achievement (Sirin meta-analysis).
- Both individual *and* school-level socioeconomic status predict achievement — with school-level SES having the *stronger* effect. The environment a teen is placed in matters more than home life alone. (PLOS One, 2025)
- Wealthier schools have better-equipped teachers, richer curricula, and stronger peer academic interaction — all raising achievement. (PLOS One, 2025)
- Lower-SES teens are about twice as likely to show learning-related behavior problems; economic stress is linked to depression and worse academic outcomes. (APA Education & SES Factsheet)

## 2. Specific scenario

**Big Brothers Big Sisters mentoring program** — a randomized controlled trial (gold-standard evidence), tracking 1,353 youth across 17 agencies over 4 years:

- Mentored youth were **42% less likely** to engage in recurring substance use.
- Mentored youth were **30% less likely** to engage in violent delinquent behavior.
- A 30-year Harvard/U.S. Treasury follow-up found mentored youth had higher college enrollment, higher salaries, and less dependence on social services later in life.
- Each mentor match costs $2,000–3,000/year but generates ~$7,000 more in lifetime tax revenue per participant — a 2-3x return.

**Why this scenario works:** same at-risk teens, same poverty, same neighborhoods — one added societal structure (a mentor) measurably reversed the odds. It isolates society as the variable.

## 4. How does AI affect teen success in education?

- Harvard RCT (physics students, *Scientific Reports*, June 2025): students using an AI tutor at home learned significantly more than students in an in-class active learning session, in less time.
- Median test scores: 4.5 (AI-tutored) vs. 3.5 (classroom) — gains more than double relative to baseline.
- Broader research: AI-supported students outscored peers by 15–35% on assessments in quasi-experimental studies, with higher engagement.
- **Access angle:** AI tutoring can level the playing field for teens without access to private tutors — turning a resource gap into a smaller one.

**Pre-empt this counterpoint:** A review of 58 studies found overreliance on AI led to superficial learning in ~65% of cases.
> Rebuttal: That's a failure of *implementation*, not of the tool. Society determines whether AI helps or harms — which loops back to the core thesis.
